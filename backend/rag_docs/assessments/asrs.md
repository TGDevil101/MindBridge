# ASRS v1.1 — Adult ADHD Self-Report Scale (Part A)

## What it is
The Adult ADHD Self-Report Scale (ASRS) was developed by the World Health Organization in collaboration with researchers at New York University. The Part A 6-item screen (Kessler et al., 2005) is the most validated brief screen for adult ADHD globally.

## What it measures
Part A asks about the past 6 months on six items:

1. How often do you have trouble wrapping up the final details of a project once the challenging parts have been done?
2. How often do you have difficulty getting things in order when you have to do a task that requires organisation?
3. How often do you have problems remembering appointments or obligations?
4. When you have a task that requires a lot of thought, how often do you avoid or delay getting started?
5. How often do you fidget or squirm with your hands or feet when you have to sit down for a long time?
6. How often do you feel overly active and compelled to do things, like you were driven by a motor?

Each item is rated 0–4:
- 0 = Never
- 1 = Rarely
- 2 = Sometimes
- 3 = Often
- 4 = Very often

## Scoring — binary screen, NO severity bands
Unlike GAD-7/PHQ-9, ASRS Part A does NOT produce a severity score. It produces a binary screen result based on threshold responses.

A response is counted as "endorsed" (1 symptom) when it meets the following thresholds:
- Items 1, 2, 3: rated "Sometimes" (2), "Often" (3), or "Very often" (4)
- Items 4, 5, 6: rated "Often" (3) or "Very often" (4)

**Note**: items 4-6 have a HIGHER threshold than items 1-3 because they describe more universal behaviours that even non-ADHD adults sometimes report.

**Result interpretation**:
- **0–3 symptoms endorsed → Screen NEGATIVE.** Symptoms inconsistent with adult ADHD. (But screen-negative does not rule out ADHD — only suggests it is less likely.)
- **4–6 symptoms endorsed → Screen POSITIVE.** Symptoms highly consistent with adult ADHD. Further clinical evaluation strongly recommended.

## CRITICAL: framing
The ASRS is a screening tool ONLY. It does NOT diagnose ADHD. A screen-positive result means "your responses are consistent with the kind of difficulties people with ADHD often report" — it does not mean you have ADHD.

Required language for MindBridge:
- "You endorsed [X] out of 6 Part A items. This is a screening result, not a diagnosis."
- For screen-positive: "Your responses are consistent with traits that can appear in ADHD. Only a clinician — typically a psychiatrist or trained psychologist — can determine whether ADHD is actually present."
- Never say "you have ADHD" or "you don't have ADHD" based on this screen.

## Why no severity bands
Adult ADHD is fundamentally a syndromic diagnosis requiring impairment criteria, developmental history (symptoms present in childhood), and exclusion of alternative explanations. The Part A screen captures only one dimension and intentionally does not pretend to estimate severity.

## Indian context
- ASRS is widely used in Indian psychiatric settings but adult ADHD is significantly underdiagnosed in India.
- Many users who screen positive in India have never been formally evaluated. A screen-positive result is often the first time someone considers ADHD as an explanation for their difficulties.
- Be careful not to over-pathologise normal stress-related concentration problems. A screen result should always be paired with "this is worth discussing with a clinician" rather than presented as definitive.
