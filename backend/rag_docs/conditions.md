# The 5 conditions MindBridge covers

Source: WHO mental health fact sheets, NIMH condition guides, NIMHANS publications.

## Anxiety
Anxiety is a normal response to stress that becomes a problem when it is persistent, excessive, and interferes with daily life. Common symptoms include constant worry, racing thoughts, muscle tension, difficulty concentrating, restlessness, and trouble sleeping. Anxiety can also produce physical symptoms — racing heart, chest tightness, shortness of breath, stomach problems. About 1 in 13 adults globally experiences an anxiety disorder. Among Indian students, exam-related anxiety and social anxiety are particularly common. Anxiety responds well to cognitive-behavioural therapy and, in moderate-to-severe cases, medication. Self-help strategies that help include regular sleep, physical activity, limiting caffeine, and grounding techniques (e.g., 5-4-3-2-1 sensory grounding).

**MindBridge uses the GAD-7 to screen for generalised anxiety symptoms.**

## Depression
Depression is more than feeling sad — it is a persistent state of low mood, loss of interest or pleasure, and reduced functioning that lasts at least two weeks and represents a change from someone's normal self. Core symptoms include low mood, loss of interest in activities previously enjoyed, fatigue, sleep disturbance, appetite changes, feelings of worthlessness or guilt, difficulty concentrating, and thoughts of death or self-harm. Globally, depression affects about 5% of adults. In India, NIMHANS National Mental Health Survey (2015-16) found that 5.25% of adults have a depressive disorder, with women, urban populations, and the 30-49 age group disproportionately affected. Depression is highly treatable through therapy (CBT, IPT), medication, or a combination. Recovery is typical when help is sought early.

**MindBridge uses the PHQ-9 to screen for depressive symptoms. Item 9 (thoughts of self-harm) triggers crisis protocol regardless of total score.**

## Stress
Stress is the body's response to demands or threats — what we feel when life feels overloaded, unpredictable, or uncontrollable. Short-term stress is normal and even useful (it sharpens focus before a presentation or exam). Chronic stress — sustained over weeks or months — is what causes problems: it suppresses immunity, disrupts sleep, contributes to anxiety and depression, and accelerates physical health issues like cardiovascular disease. Symptoms of chronic stress include constant tension, irritability, sleep problems, racing thoughts, difficulty concentrating, and feeling overwhelmed even by small tasks. Among Indian students, academic pressure, family expectations, and competition for limited educational/career opportunities are major stressors. Effective coping involves regular routine, physical activity, social connection, breaks from the source of stress, and learning that "doing more" is rarely the answer when stress is high.

**MindBridge uses the PSS-10 to screen for perceived stress levels.**

## ADHD
Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder is a neurodevelopmental condition characterised by persistent patterns of inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity that interfere with functioning. ADHD is NOT a deficit of attention — it is dysregulation of attention. People with ADHD often hyperfocus on interesting tasks while struggling with mundane ones. Symptoms include difficulty sustaining attention on uninteresting tasks, frequent careless errors, difficulty organising work or following multi-step instructions, losing things, distractibility, restlessness, fidgeting, talking excessively, difficulty waiting one's turn, and acting before thinking. ADHD usually begins in childhood but is often diagnosed only in adulthood, especially in women and in people whose childhood symptoms were less disruptive. ADHD is significantly underdiagnosed in India. It is highly treatable through stimulant medication, therapy, accommodations, and structural strategies. The ASRS Part A is a brief screening tool — diagnosis requires a clinician.

**MindBridge uses the ASRS v1.1 Part A as a binary screen for ADHD traits.**

## Loneliness
Loneliness is the painful subjective experience of feeling disconnected from others — feeling unheard, unseen, or alone even in the presence of others. It is distinct from solitude (which can be positive) and from depression (though they can overlap). Loneliness is associated with significant health risks at high levels: increased cardiovascular disease, weaker immune function, faster cognitive decline, and elevated risk of depression. It affects all age groups but is particularly common during life transitions — moving to a new city, starting college, becoming a parent, retirement, bereavement. Post-COVID, loneliness rates rose significantly in young Indian adults. Loneliness is responsive to small, consistent efforts at connection: regular contact with even one person, shared activities, and gradually re-engaging with social groups. It is NOT a clinical disorder. The PDF specification explicitly mandates warm, connection-focused language for MindBridge responses about loneliness — never catastrophising.

**MindBridge uses the UCLA-3 to screen for loneliness levels.**
